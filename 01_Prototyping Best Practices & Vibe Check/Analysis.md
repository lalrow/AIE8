1. Explain the concept of object-oriented programming in simple terms to a complete beginner. 
Sure! Object-oriented programming (OOP) is a way to write code that organizes things like objects in the real world. Imagine you want to create a video game with animals. In OOP, you create a blueprint called a "class" for an animal. This blueprint says what an animal has (like a name, color, or age) and what it can do (like run or make a sound). Then, you create actual "objects" from this blueprint. For example, a dog object and a cat object. Each object has its own information but follows the same blueprint.

4. If a store sells apples in packs of 4 and oranges in packs of 3, how many packs of each do I need to buy to get exactly 12 apples and 9 oranges?

You want exactly 12 apples and 9 oranges.

- Apples come in packs of 4.
- Oranges come in packs of 3.

To get exactly 12 apples:

Number of apple packs × 4 = 12  
Number of apple packs = 12 ÷ 4 = 3 packs

To get exactly 9 oranges:

Number of orange packs × 3 = 9  
Number of orange packs = 9 ÷ 3 = 3 packs

**Answer:** You need to buy 3 packs of apples and 3 packs of oranges.

Response:
The sky appears blue due to a phenomenon known as Rayleigh scattering, which occurs when sunlight passes through Earth's atmosphere and interacts with minute particles and gas molecules. When white sunlight encounters these small particles, it scatters in various directions; however, blue light, having a shorter wavelength than other colors such as red, yellow, or green, is scattered to a greater extent. Consequently, the sky is perceived as blue during daylight hours. This scattering process is named after Lord Rayleigh, who first provided a scientific explanation for it in the late 19th century.

Sunlight comprises a spectrum of colors, as evidenced when it passes through a prism or manifests as a rainbow following rain. The atmosphere functions analogously to a prism, scattering different colors in varying degrees. Both blue and violet light experience the most scattering due to their shorter wavelengths, though human vision is more sensitive to blue light, leading to the predominant perception of a blue sky rather than violet.

During sunrise and sunset, the sky often takes on red or orange hues because sunlight travels through a thicker layer of the atmosphere, resulting in most of the blue light being scattered out before reaching the observer; thus, the remaining light is predominantly red and orange. This effect also causes the sun itself to appear red or orange at these times. The same principle explains why distant mountains may appear blue or purple, as increased atmospheric distance causes more blue light to scatter toward the observer.

On planets with atmospheres differing from Earth's, the sky would exhibit distinctly different colors. For instance, Mars displays a butterscotch-colored sky during the day, attributable to the iron oxide dust suspended in its atmosphere. While the blue coloration of Earth's sky is essentially an optical effect resulting from atmospheric interaction with sunlight, it remains one of the most striking and beautiful natural phenomena observable on clear days.
